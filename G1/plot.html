Analysis

Echoing my sentiments in the MLP N' Friends hub page, I generally feel that the plot direction of My Little Pony N' Friends to be quite engaging as an adult viewer. Granted, I generally enjoy children's cartoons, so I may have some bias. Regardless, thinking from the perspective of a outsider, I feel that generation 1 is generally more approachable than the more 'girly' iterations of generation 2 and 3. While the show has and still is targeted towards young girls with the intent to sell toys, the more adventure oriented plot combined with fantasy elements may engage both parents and siblings of a young girl watching this show.

I feel that it is still nonetheless important to consider this under the lens of gender roles between the 80s and now. Generally speaking, consciousness about social causes such as feminism and intersectionality are much further along today compared to the 80s. Cartoons being 'for girls' or 'for boys' was a commonplace idea. Nowadays however, cartoons are categorized a lot more loosely and it is not an oddity for girls to watch shows targeted towards boys or vice versa.

All this is to say, that considering the climate of feminism, consciousness of sexism and the dissolution of the once rigid barriers between genders, it is remarkable that a show from this era went to such lengths to make it approachable to boys and parents as well. This is a good thing, as the show uses its plot devices as a vehicle to teach children important values such as respect, tolerance, equality, friendship, and love. The creator of generation 4, Lauren Faust, has also cited teaching Love and Tolerance as one of the key goals of the series.

Personally speaking, I find this quite important. While the show was intended to sell toys, its important that media that we give children serves more of a purpose than a vehicle for advertisement and profit.

In conclusion, I found My Little Pony N' Friends to be a surprising and lovely first creation in a series that I love.
